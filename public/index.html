The Process

I follow an industry standard process set out by one of the leading companies that utilises UX in almost all parts of their business - Google. The following approach is a general example of how I work but it is worth mentioning that each project is unique. Therefore this process is being altered depending on project needs.

Stage 1: Empathise with Users

There are number of techniques I like and have used across different projects.

Photo and Video User-based Studies. Photographing or recording target users, like other empathising methods, helps uncover needs that people have which they may or may not be aware of.
Interviews: Interviews are an important part of the UX designer‚Äôs skillset for empathising with users. However, an interview can yield only minimal results if not conducted with genuine empathy.
Analogous Empathy: Using analogies can help developing new insights. By comparing one domain with another, we as designers can conjure different solutions that would not necessarily come to mind when working within the constraints of one discipline.

Stage 2: Define

In the Define stage I synthesise the observations about the users from the first stage.

Problem Statement: A good problem statement should be: User-centred, broad enough for creative freedom but narrow enough to make it manageable.
Empathy Map: An empathy map consists of four quadrants laid out on a board, paper or table, which reflect the four key traits that the users demonstrated/possessed during the observation stage. The four quadrants refer to what the users: Said, Did, Thought, and Felt.

Stage 3: Ideate

In the Ideation stage, design thinkers spark off ideas ‚Äî in the form of questions and solutions ‚Äî through creative and curious activities such as Brainstorms and Worst Possible Idea.

Some of the methods I have used to spark innovative ideas are: Brainstorming, Storyboarding and Mind-mapping;

Stage 4: Prototype

I‚Äôm experienced in both Low-Fidelity and High-Fidelity Prototyping using tools such as Miro, Adobe XD and Figma.

Stage 5: Test

Through testing, I learn more about the users, improve my prototypes and even refine the problem statement. During testing I always try to follow the following guidelines:

- Show, don‚Äôt tell: let your people experience the prototype.
- Ask test participants to talk through their experience.
- Observe the users.
- Ask follow-up questions.

And, last but not least, the design thinking process is fluid, iterative and flexible: the different stages often feed into one another and don‚Äôt necessarily follow any fixed sequence. That said, the ideal end point of design thinking (when you know you‚Äôve done a great job) is when the product or service is desirable, feasible and viable.
